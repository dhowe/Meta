Mini Grammar 2:

Weather Forecast

This piece illustrates the flexibility of context free grammars in generating language. It is up to the artist to create beauty, computer is only an aid.

This sketch represents a weather forecast program starting from Sunday. To make the program more interesting, the reporter adds comments and advices to the audience, as a way to enhance communication. Phrases and sentences are carefully chosen to have a unified tone from the beginning to the end. Breaking news taken from CNN.com.

Interstate 95

“Interstate 95” uses text and image grammars to tell an enigmatic story. I found photos by searching Google Images with terms “night,” “rain,” “car,” etc., and I organized them by level of focus. Thus as the words get more abstract, so do the images. I wanted to convey the feeling of losing consciousness, whether by drifting into imagination or careening towards disaster.

Sunset on Thayer Street

My piece enacts the process of remembering a striking but ambiguous moment. As the words change, the timelapse photography fast-forwards, rewinds, and jumps back and forth, mirroring the movement of the memory.

Notes: Turn speakers on and be patient, as it takes a minute to load. The numbers on the Java console indicate the number of images loaded (82 is max). The piece runs for 2 minutes.

Music: Introduction from "Knife in the Water" by Kryzstof Komeda.

Iteration 1:

Tragi-Comic

Sketch that generates a series of mini-comics based on the Clue game characters and scenarios. As the user clicks and the comic progresses, all but one character eventually die off, and the remaining character "is alone."

I wanted to play with the idea of narratives that have their own "grammars" in the same way that simple sentences have grammars. The rather fixed set of possibilities inherent to the Clue game worked in this way, but I'd like to use the form of a comic and the grammar of narrative in a more complex way for my next iteration of this project.

Iteration 2:

Cluedo Grammar

The progression of the texts from sense to nonsense is meant to reflect the way memories, especially tragic or shocking memories, often change meaning or lose meaning over time. Words in the text are replaced with other, similar sounding words, at first slowly, then very quickly. Since the replacements are sorted by how similar they sound, verbs often change tense, which messes with the temporality of the narrative. A name will change in one place and not another, destroying any sense of constant identity.

I find it interesting to try and locate the point at which the text totally loses meaning, but, since the texts are long and the program moves quickly, this process often begins before one can even finish reading over the original.

Iteration # 1

Sketch that generates, based on the given grammar rules, words and/or phrases in random locations on the screen. When clicked the strings will shift to a new string from the grammar rules. Pressing a key will randomize all the strings. Will eventually be updated to make use of a webcam to allow for a more active user interaction. It would allow users to change the message of the piece and leave it for the people who are yet to use the piece. In a way, the users determine the message of the piece instead of the piece itself.
