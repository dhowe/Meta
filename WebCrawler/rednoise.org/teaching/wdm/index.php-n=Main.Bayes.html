Bayes’ Theorem     (example from dan shiffman's Programming A-Z)

Bayes’ Rule 

  p(A|B) = (p(B|A) * p(A))  / (p(B|A) * p(A) + p(B|~A) * p(~A))

Consider the following scenario:

  1. 1% of students living in Providence are afflicted with a rare disease known as Providosis.
  2. There is a test you can take to determine if you have it, known as a TFP (Test For Providosis).
  3. 90% of all students with Providosis will receive a positivie TFP (i.e. 10% that have the disease will receive a false negative).
  4. 95% of students without Providosis will receive a negative TFP (i.e. 5% will receive false positives).

You have received a positive TFP, what is the likelihood you have Providosis?

Strangely enough, there is a very precise answer to this question and it’s not what you would expect. Bayesian reasoning is counter-intuitive and takes quite a bit of getting used to. In fact, when given a similar question related to breast cancer and mammograms, only 15% of doctors get the answer correct.

The answer — 15.3% — is calculated via Bayes’ Theorem. Let’s look at it again with this scenario:

  1. There are 1000 students.
  2. 10 of them have Providosis.
  3. 9 of those 10 with the disease will receive a positive TFP.
  4. Out of the 990 w/o Providosis, ~50 will receive positive TFPs.
  5. Therefore, 59 total students receive positive TFPs, 9 of which actually have the disease, 50 do not.
  6. The chance one has the disease if the test is positive is therefore 9 / 58 = 15.5% (off slightly from the exact result b/c of rounding)

The problem our brains run into are those rascally 90% and 95% numbers. 90% of students who test positive have the disease and 95% who don’t test negative, if I test positive, I should probably have it, right?!! The important thing to remember is that only 1% of students actually have the disease. Sure testing positive increases the likelihood, but because 5% of students without the disease receive a false positive, it only increases the chances to 15%. All of this is explained on Dan Shiffmans's pages on Bayesian analysis and more deeply in thorough and wonderful detail in Eliezer Yudkowsky’s [[Readings | article]. An Intuitive Explanation of Bayesian Reasoning.

By the way, we could have calculated it as follows:

P (Providosis | Positive TFP) = (90% * 1%) / (90% * 1% + 5% * 99%).

This reads as “the probability that a positive TFP means you have Providosis” equals. . . .

So why do we care? This type of reasoning can be applied quite nicely to text analysis. The example we read about this week was spam filtering; if we know the probability that a spam e-mail contains certain words and that non-spam e-mails contain certain words, we can calculate the likelihood that an e-mail is spam based on what words it contains. In what other contexts might we apply this (and similar) techniques?
