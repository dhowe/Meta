Mine
Is anonymous truly anonymous? Are we safe in confessing our personal thoughts to any website, despite its promises to protect our privacy?
Mine is an exploration of the volatile relationship between two of these sites (fml.com, grouphug.us), the users who post on them, and the spectator at home. By exposing the only component that had previously granted the user with the courage to broadcast their private confessions, it becomes more apparent that the number of ways that our privacy can be violated are countless and unrestrained.

Let Me Illustrate That For You
This piece is meant to be a playful challenge to the viewer to decipher the connection between the text and the images. The text is drawn from the most recent postings to the popular site, My Life is Average, and the images are queried (via URL manipulation) from Bing Images. The images are sometimes eerily fitting and on other occasions, seemingly unrelated.
To see a new message and image, click the applet.

Userscrape
Enter your username. The piece will display text from webpages you authored or contributed to under your username, along with pictures associated with your username by Google Images. The text is draggable. Press space for a new portrait of your web presence and pray it doesn't find your old xanga.
Userscrape 2.0
In the second iteration, you can mouse over a line of text to show what site the text is from. This iteration also adds a temporal element - as time goes on, the text comes from pages that go deeper into the Google results, becoming older and less relevant but perhaps revealing new truths.
Userscrape 3.0
For our final iteration, we improved the visuals to better reflect the dynamic, temporal nature of the program. As the words and phrases become less relevant, the images are slowly clouded by the remains of the text until they become illegible. Finally, the text is more personally relevant so that the shift is perceptible.

Creative Search
Creative search is designed to help you look for something when you aren't sure what you're looking for. It is a brainstorming, creativity-support tool. In this first iteration, users can start a search, and branch off new searches. Results are displayed in a graph, and rapidly expand away from your query so that you can quickly explore relevant terms and refine what it is you are looking for. If a relevant image is found for the query, it is displayed as well. Instructions and code are provided on the linked applet page.
Crying Out Load (COL)
We decided to go a different direction for our second iteration. Crying Out Loud attempts to make the viewer laugh, by presenting light-hearted combinations of cute animals and all-too-serious snippets pulled from blogs on the web. We couldn't help but smile given some of the ridiculous, yet seemingly-appropriate juxtapositions the piece outputs.
Crying Out Load (COL) 2.0
In our third iteration, we wanted to bridge the gap between playful and funny and serious and depressing. We did this in two ways. First, we included a button labeled, "Help." When viewers click on this button, they are taken to the page where the captions for this piece are scraped from. At this moment, the viewer suddenly becomes aware of the source of the text in the images, a sobering experience. Another way in which viewers can gain this insight is by visiting the gallery. This page is designed such that COLs (Crying Out Loud pieces) are displayed in-between our opposing sources, so that the extreme juxtaposition becomes more clear. In addition to these changes, we implemented a host of technical improvements, such as asynchronous loading, auto-save to gallery with database integration, and more.

The Internet Learning Tool
For this project I wanted to do something that highlighted the vast amount of meaningless, misinformed drivel that pervades the Internet. I also wanted to draw attention to the subjective nature of Wikipedia (which also seemed like an interesting site to scrape). Thus the Internet Learning Tool was born. Users look up a term using the GUI and the tool pulls the wikipedia page for that term. Then, it replaces random sentences from the article with random sentences from the article for that term on encyclopediadramatica.com (a hilariously vulgar and comprehensive compendium of memes and other useless internet knowledge). The end result is a vaguely familar, usually nonsensical, often extremely offensive shadow of the original article. Note that this applet is mostly just a way to test the sentence replacing; I'd like to implement this as a stand-alone website given a little more time.
